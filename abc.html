Chapter 10: Analysis of Water and Wastewater

Overview of Important Concepts:

  • Standardization: To ensure legal and scientific validity, water analysis relies on "Standard Methods" approved by agencies like the EPA.
  • Sampling: The accuracy of an analysis is limited by the quality of the sample. Key concepts include grab samples (specific time/place) vs. composite samples (averages over time) and proper preservation (usually refrigeration at $4^\circ\text{C}$).
  • Indicators: Because testing for every pathogen is impossible, coliform bacteria are used as indicators of fecal contamination.
  • Spectroscopy: Light absorption (Beer’s Law) is used to measure concentrations of nutrients and metals. UV-Visible is used for molecules; Atomic Absorption (AAS) and ICP are used for metals.
  • Chromatography: Ion Chromatography (IC) is the standard for measuring anions ($Cl^-$, $NO_3^-$, $SO_4^{2-}$), while GC and HPLC are used for organics.
  • Electrochemistry: Sensors like pH electrodes and conductivity probes provide rapid, real-time data on water quality.

Questions and Solutions

1. List two different agencies that issue analytical methods that are used for the analysis of water and wastewater.

  • Answer:
    1. The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA).
    2. The American Public Health Association (APHA), which publishes Standard Methods for the Examination of Water and Wastewater.

2. Describe the process by which a standard method of analysis is certified.

  • Answer: A method becomes a "standard method" through collaborative testing (also known as round-robin testing). The same sample is sent to many different certified laboratories. The proposed method is performed by many different analysts to determine its reproducibility and accuracy. Once reviewed and validated by a committee, it is designated a standard method.

3. How are the EPA methods of analysis categorized?

  • Answer: They are categorized by analyte (the substance being measured) and matrix (the medium containing the analyte, such as drinking water, wastewater, or solid waste). Each method is assigned a specific number (e.g., Method 1600 for bacteria).

4. Define the following:

  • Answer:
    • a. Analyte: The specific chemical substance or component that is being measured in a sample.
    • b. Matrix: The medium (water, soil, sludge, etc.) that contains the analyte.

5. What is meant by “obtaining a representative sample”?

  • Answer: Obtaining a sample that accurately reflects the true composition of the larger body of water at that time.
    • a. What is a grab sample? A single sample collected at a specific location and a specific time.
    • b. What is a composite sample? A mixture of multiple grab samples collected at the same point at different times (or different points at the same time) to give an average value.
    • c. What is an analyte? (See Q4a).

6. What is the most common way to preserve a sample?

  • Answer: Refrigeration to $4^\circ\text{C}$ is the most common method. It slows biological activity and chemical reactions without altering the phase of the sample (like freezing might).

7. The coliform bacteria count is used to test water for which type of contamination?

  • Answer: It tests for fecal contamination.
    • a. Do coliform bacteria cause disease? Generally, no. They are indicator organisms found naturally in the human intestine, but their presence suggests pathogens might be present.
    • b. What is the EPA maximum contaminant level? For drinking water, the goal is zero. Regulatory action limits are often set at 4 coliform colonies per 100 mL for specific tests (membrane filtration).
    • c. How do you treat water that contains pathogens? Disinfection methods such as chlorination, ozone, or UV radiation.

8. Describe the multiple-tube fermentation technique for coliform bacteria.

  • Answer: Five test tubes filled with a lactose broth are inoculated with water samples and incubated at $35^\circ\text{C}$ for 48 hours. If coliforms are present, they ferment the lactose, producing gas or acid. The results are reported statistically as the Most Probable Number (MPN) of organisms.

9. Describe the membrane filtration technique for coliform bacteria.

  • Answer: A large volume of water (e.g., 100 mL) is passed through a sterile membrane filter with a pore size small enough to trap bacteria. The filter is placed on a nutrient agar plate and incubated. Coliform colonies grow on the surface and are counted directly using a microscope.

10. Describe the ortho-nitrophenyl-$\beta$-D-galactopyranoside (ONPG) test for coliform bacteria.

  • Answer: This is a rapid enzymatic assay. Coliform bacteria possess the enzyme $\beta$-D-galactosidase. When the ONPG substrate is added to the sample, this enzyme hydrolyzes it, releasing a chromogen that turns the liquid yellow. A yellow color indicates a positive result.

11. Draw a schematic of the Clark oxygen sensor and label all parts. Write the reaction that:

  • Answer:

[Image of Clark Oxygen Sensor]

* **a. Occurs at the cathode:** Oxygen reduction: $O_2 + 4H^+ + 4e^- \rightarrow 2H_2O$.

12. The TOC measurement assesses which water-quality parameter?

  • Answer: Total Organic Carbon.
    • a. How are TOC measurements made? Organic carbon is oxidized to $CO_2$ using heat/oxygen, UV radiation, or chemical oxidants. The $CO_2$ produced is then measured (often by Infrared spectroscopy).
    • b. How can the results of the TOC test differ from the BOD? TOC measures all organic carbon, including non-biodegradable plastics or chemicals. BOD measures only the oxygen consumed by bacteria to degrade biologically available organics.
    • c. Which detectors are used on the TOC instrument? A Non-Dispersive Infrared (NDIR) detector is typically used to measure the $CO_2$ gas produced.

13. Describe how to measure the BOD.

  • Answer: A water sample is aerated to saturation. The initial Dissolved Oxygen (DO) is measured. The sample is sealed in a bottle and incubated in the dark at $20^\circ\text{C}$ for 5 days. The final DO is measured. The BOD is the difference: $\text{Initial DO} - \text{Final DO}$.

14. Arrange the following in order of increasing energy: X-rays, infrared light, UV light, visible light, microwaves.

  • Answer: Microwaves \< Infrared light \< Visible light \< UV light \< X-rays.

15. Convert the following absorbance (A) measurements into percent transmittance (%T).

  • Concept: $A = 2 - \log(\%T)$ or $\%T = 10^{2-A}$
    • a. 0.065: $10^{2-0.065} = \textbf{86.1\%}$
    • b. 0.320: $10^{2-0.320} = \textbf{47.9\%}$
    • c. 0.425: $10^{2-0.425} = \textbf{37.6\%}$

16. Convert the following transmittance measurements into absorbance (A).

  • Concept: $A = -\log(T)$ where T is decimal transmittance.
    • a. 22.6%: $A = -\log(0.226) = \textbf{0.646}$
    • b. 31.4%: $A = -\log(0.314) = \textbf{0.503}$
    • c. 0.102: $A = -\log(0.102) = \textbf{0.991}$

    • If %T = 10, A = 1.0
    • If A = 0.5, %T = 31.6
    • If %T = 50, A = 0.301

18. A solution containing 4.5 ppm of anthracene has a transmittance of 0.360 in a 1-cm cell at a wavelength of 280 nm. Calculate the molar absorptivity, $\epsilon$. Indicate $\epsilon$’s units.

  • Answer:
    1. Convert 4.5 ppm (mg/L) to Molarity. MW of Anthracene ($C_{14}H_{10}$) = 178.23 g/mol. $M = \frac{4.5 \times 10^{-3} \text{ g/L}}{178.23 \text{ g/mol}} = 2.52 \times 10^{-5} \text{ mol/L}$.
    2. Calculate A: $A = -\log(0.360) = 0.444$.
    3. Beer's Law ($A = \epsilon bc$): $\epsilon = \frac{A}{bc} = \frac{0.444}{1 \text{ cm} \times 2.52 \times 10^{-5} \text{ M}} = \textbf{17,619 } M^{-1}cm^{-1}$.

19. Iodine Deficiency Analysis: * a. Calculate Molar Absorptivity: $A = 0.240$, $c = 3.5 \times 10^{-6} M$. $\epsilon = \frac{0.240}{3.5 \times 10^{-6}} = \textbf{68,571 } M^{-1}cm^{-1}$. * b. Calculate Concentration of Unknown: $A = 0.140$. $c = \frac{A}{\epsilon} = \frac{0.140}{68,571} = \textbf{2.04 \times 10^{-6} M}$.

20. A colored substance Z... * a. Calculate absorptivity: $C = 2 \text{ mg/L} = 0.002 \text{ g/L}$. $A = 0.850$. $a = \frac{A}{bc} = \frac{0.850}{1 \times 0.002} = \textbf{425 L g}^{-1}\text{cm}^{-1}$. * b. Calculate concentration: $A = 0.450$. $C = \frac{A}{ab} = \frac{0.450}{425 \times 1} = 0.00106 \text{ g/L} = \textbf{1.06 ppm}$.

21. The un-ionized form of weak acid HA does not absorb... What is the approximate pKa?

  • Answer: The absorbance increases as the acid ionizes ($HA \rightarrow H^+ + A^-$). The maximum absorbance is 0.595 (fully ionized). The pKa occurs when the acid is 50% ionized, meaning absorbance is half of the max ($0.595 / 2 \approx 0.30$). Looking at the data table provided in the text context (implied), an absorbance of \~0.30 occurs between pH 6.0 and 7.0. The approximate pKa is 6.4.

22. Draw a schematic of a single-beam UV-visible spectrometer.

  • Answer: Source $\rightarrow$ Monochromator $\rightarrow$ Sample $\rightarrow$ Detector.
    • a. Which lamp is used for the visible region? Tungsten lamp.
    • b. Which lamp is used for the UV region? Deuterium arc lamp.
    • c. Can glass cells be used? No, glass absorbs UV light. Quartz cells must be used.

23. Draw a schematic of a double-beam UV-visible spectrometer.

  • Answer:

[Image of double-beam spectrometer] Source $\rightarrow$ Monochromator $\rightarrow$ Chopper (Splitter) $\rightarrow$ Sample/Reference $\rightarrow$ Detector. * a. What is the function of the chopper? It alternately directs light through the sample and reference cells, allowing the instrument to compare them continuously. * b. How does this instrument correct for changes? It continuously subtracts the reference (blank) signal from the sample signal, canceling out fluctuations in lamp intensity.

24. Describe the spectrophotometric method used to determine phosphorus.

  • Answer: Phosphate reacts with ammonium molybdate and antimony potassium tartrate in an acid medium to form an antimony-phosphomolybdate complex. This is reduced by ascorbic acid to form an intensely blue-colored complex ("Molybdenum Blue"). The intensity of the blue color is proportional to the phosphorus concentration.

25. Phosphorus Calculation:

  • a. Molar Absorptivity: $\epsilon = \frac{0.434}{3.42 \times 10^{-5}} = \textbf{12,690 } M^{-1}cm^{-1}$.
  • b. Concentration of Unknown: Diluted conc: $C_{dil} = \frac{0.780}{12,690} = 6.15 \times 10^{-5} M$. Original conc (Dilution factor = 250/10 = 25): $C_{orig} = 6.15 \times 10^{-5} \times 25 = \textbf{1.54 \times 10^{-3} M}$.

26. Draw a schematic of an ion chromatograph.

  • Answer:

[Image of Ion Chromatograph] Pump $\rightarrow$ Injector $\rightarrow$ Column $\rightarrow$ Suppressor $\rightarrow$ Conductivity Detector. * a. Introduction: Via a sample injection loop/valve. * b. Resin: For anions, the resin is positively charged (quaternary ammonium groups). * c. Detector: Conductivity detector, because ions conduct electricity.

27. Predict the order of elution of the following ions from a quaternary ammonium anion-exchange resin: $I^-, F^-, Br^-, NO_3^-, SO_4^{2-}$.

  • Answer: Ions with lower charge and smaller size (lower affinity) elute first. Order: $F^-$, $Cl^-$, $Br^-$, $NO_3^-$, $SO_4^{2-}$. (Note: $I^-$ usually elutes later due to high affinity/polarizability, often after nitrate/sulfate depending on conditions).

28. Describe the function of the suppressor in ion chromatography.

  • Answer: The suppressor reduces the background conductivity of the eluent (mobile phase) while increasing the conductivity of the analyte ions. It exchanges the eluent cation (e.g., $Na^+$) for $H^+$, converting conductive NaOH eluent into water, and converting analyte salts into highly conductive acids.

29. Referring to Figure 10.13 (in text), estimate the concentration...

  • Answer: (This requires visual comparison to the standard peaks). Generally, you compare the peak height or area of the unknown to the standard. If the unknown peak is half the size of a 50 ppm standard, the concentration is 25 ppm.

30. Describe the easiest way to measure total dissolved solids, total ionized solids, or salinity.

  • Answer: Conductivity measurement. The ability of the water to conduct an electric current is directly proportional to the concentration of dissolved ions.

31. Define the following: * a. Resistivity: The resistance of the solution to the flow of current (measured in ohms). * b. Conductivity: The reciprocal of resistivity (measured in microsiemens per cm, $\mu S/cm$).

32. A solution has a conductivity of 200 mS/m. Convert this value to $\mu$mhos/cm.

  • Answer: $1 \text{ mS/m} = 10 \mu\text{mhos/cm}$. $200 \times 10 = \textbf{2000 } \mu\text{mhos/cm}$.

33. Which is the most mobile cation in solution? How much more efficient is this cation?

  • Answer: The Hydrogen ion ($H^+$). It is about 5 times more efficient/mobile than other cations due to the "proton hopping" mechanism.

34. Which is the most mobile anion in solution? How much more efficient is this anion?

  • Answer: The Hydroxide ion ($OH^-$). It is about 3 times more efficient than other anions.

35. How is the salinity of natural waters measured?

  • Answer: By measuring conductivity and comparing it to the conductivity of standard seawater.

36. Describe the construction of the pH electrode.

  • Answer:

[Image of pH electrode] It consists of a thin, pH-sensitive glass bulb containing a buffer solution and an internal reference electrode (Ag/AgCl). When dipped in a sample, a potential difference develops across the glass membrane proportional to the difference in pH between the inner and outer solutions.

37. Describe the construction of the calcium ion electrode.

  • Answer: It uses a liquid ion exchanger (a hydrophobic membrane containing a calcium-binding organic molecule like calcium didecylphosphate). This membrane selectively binds $Ca^{2+}$ ions, creating a potential difference relative to a reference electrode.

38. Calcium Electrode Calculation:

  • a. Calibration Curve: Plot Potential (mV) vs. log[Ca].
  • b. Sample 1 (+16 mV): Using the Nernst equation slope ($\approx$ 29.6 mV per decade for divalent ions), calculate concentration. (Specific answer requires calibration data points, but typically follows logarithmic relationship).
  • c. Sample 2 (-38 mV): Same calculation. Lower potential indicates lower concentration.

39. Describe how you would calibrate a pH electrode.

  • Answer: Use at least two standard buffer solutions that bracket the expected pH of the sample. Common standards are pH 4.01 and pH 7.00 (or pH 10.00). Adjust the meter to read these values.

40. The sensitivity of the $Ca^{2+}$ electrode is 50 times greater for Ca than Mg...

  • Answer:
    • a. 3% Error by Mg: Since sensitivity is 50:1, you need 50 times as much Mg to create the same signal as Ca. To cause a 3% error, the Mg concentration would need to be $50 \times 0.03 = 1.5$ times the Ca concentration.
    • b. 2% Error by Na: Sensitivity is 1000:1. Na concentration would need to be $1000 \times 0.02 = 20$ times the Ca concentration.

41. Define the following for a water sample:

  • Answer:
    • a. Alkalinity: The acid-neutralizing capacity (ANC) of water; the sum of bases (bicarbonate, carbonate, hydroxide) capable of accepting protons.
    • b. ANC: Acid Neutralizing Capacity (essentially synonymous with alkalinity).
    • c. Acidity: The base-neutralizing capacity; the capacity of water to donate protons.
    • d. Hardness: The concentration of multivalent cations, primarily Calcium ($Ca^{2+}$) and Magnesium ($Mg^{2+}$).

42. Why is the measurement of pH in natural waters an intensity factor and alkalinity a capacity factor?

  • Answer: pH measures the immediate concentration of $H^+$ ions (intensity). Alkalinity measures the reservoir of bases available to buffer against changes in pH (capacity to resist acidification).

43. What is NOM?

  • Answer: Natural Organic Matter (like humic and fulvic acids).
    • a. Sites: It has many carboxyl and phenolic proton-accepting sites.
    • b. Typical compound: Humic Acid.

44. Using equations, show how alkalinity differs from pH.

  • Answer: $pH = -\log[H^+]$ $\text{Alkalinity} \approx [HCO_3^-] + 2[CO_3^{2-}] + [OH^-] - [H^+]$ (pH depends only on $H^+$; Alkalinity depends on the sum of bases).

45. What are the pKa values for the following:

  • Answer:
    • a. $B(OH)_4^-$: pKa $\approx$ 9.2
    • b. $HPO_4^{2-}$: pKa $\approx$ 12.3
    • c. $HS^-$: pKa $\approx$ 19 (very high, rarely acts as acid in water).

46. Two water samples are collected...

  • a. Compare Alkalinity: The sample with higher concentrations of carbonate/bicarbonate has higher alkalinity.
  • b. Well-buffered: The sample with higher alkalinity is better buffered against acid rain.

47. Seawater has a pH of 8...

  • Answer: At pH 8, alkalinity is dominated by bicarbonate ($HCO_3^-$). If $[HCO_3^-] + [CO_3^{2-}] = 2 \text{ mM}$, the alkalinity is approximately 2 meq/L. Yes, seawater is well-buffered.

48. The hardness of water can be determined by cation ion chromatography.

  • Answer:
    • a. Measure: $Ca^{2+}$ and $Mg^{2+}$.
    • b. Resin: Cation-exchange resin (negatively charged, sulfonated).
    • c. Suppressor: Anion exchange membrane that swaps eluent acid for water.
    • d. Detector: Conductivity detector.

49. How are radioactive substances that may be present in water detected?

  • Answer: By evaporating water and using a proportional counter to detect alpha and beta particles, or using a gamma spectrometer to detect gamma rays.

50. Describe the following instruments:

  • Answer:
    • a. $\gamma$ Spectrometer: Uses a solid-state detector (like Germanium) to measure the specific energy of emitted gamma rays, allowing identification of specific isotopes.
    • b. Proportional Counter: A gas-filled tube where incoming radiation ionizes the gas. The electrical pulse produced is proportional to the energy of the particle, allowing alpha and beta particles to be distinguished.
